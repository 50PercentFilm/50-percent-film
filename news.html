Sixteen years on, David Fincher and Aaron Sorkin’s film looks less like a docudrama and more like a prophecy — and with Sorkin’s upcoming sequel The Social Reckoning on the horizon, its relevance has never been sharper.

“The internet isn’t written in pencil, Mark. It’s written in ink.”

Picture this: it’s 2009, Facebook has recently overtaken MySpace as the world’s most popular social media site, and news breaks that the director of Fight Club, Seven, and Zodiac, David Fincher, is set to direct a movie about the platform. Understandably, there might have been some confusion. Imagine if today we heard that Christopher Nolan’s follow-up to Oppenheimer and The Odyssey is to be ‘the TikTok movie’ — it sounds inconceivable, but that’s more or less what happened some seventeen years ago with the announcement of Fincher’s eighth picture, The Social Network. On the surface, a docudrama recounting the twin lawsuits Facebook creator Mark Zuckerberg found himself up against: one from Tyler and Cameron Winklevoss (Armie Hammer) and Divya Narendra (Max Minghella), and the other from former best friend and Facebook co-creator Eduardo Saverin (Andrew Garfield). But as the film unfolds, Aaron Sorkin’s screenplay blossoms into something much more — a thrilling, and ultimately correct, character study of Mark Zuckerberg and the generation of chronically online individuals his actions would go on to create. It blew expectations away on release, and now plays as a definitive 21st-century text that somehow saw the future coming.

The long and short of why I think this film is so special — and why I believe it to be the most important of the century — is simple. Christopher Nolan spent his Oppenheimer press tour telling us, correctly, that it was J. Robert Oppenheimer’s world we were living in. I’d argue it is, just as much, Mark Zuckerberg’s. Social media — which has completely redefined what it means to be alive in the 21st century — can technically be dated back to 1997 and the launch of Andrew Weinreich’s SixDegrees, the first site of its kind. But it isn’t until the digital boom of the post-Matrix 2000s that day-to-day human experience becomes as much online as not, and nobody is more directly responsible for that than Zuckerberg himself. Vanity Fair placed Zuckerberg at number one on its 2010 New Establishment list of the hundred most influential figures in the world. Tom Anderson and Chris DeWolfe, the creators of MySpace, certainly had a say too — MySpace popularised the virtual community experience on a mainstream scale, peaking at 115 million global monthly visitors in 2008 — but their reign would ultimately give way to the cleaner, grander, more powerful experience offered by Facebook. MySpace effectively stopped being a major social network around 2011, while Facebook and its legion of finger-scrolling offspring went on to determine what successful socialising looked like for the rest of the century. No longer defined by your ability to handle close encounters of the face-to-face kind, but by how many likes your latest status got, how many ‘friends’ you had, or — if you really had something special — whether your relationship status was on display for the entire world to see. A whole new social currency, and one that became the defining factor in deciding social standing, particularly for those young, impressionable, and in education — as we learn in The Social Network, Zuckerberg’s initial target market.

Reshaping human existence in this way, in my view, makes Zuckerberg just as influential a figure as the father of the atomic bomb himself — each man fundamentally remodelling the consequences of bringing life into a world after his respective, ruinous invention. What makes The Social Network such a critical 21st-century text is that, despite the full consequences of Zuckerberg’s actions not yet being clear in 2010, Fincher and Sorkin manage, in just two hours, to lay bare the story of why and how the world is the way it is today — before that world had even fully arrived. Sixteen years on, the film looks almost prophetic in a society whose dependency on the digital landscape it depicts has only escalated since release, perhaps best epitomised by a line spoken by Justin Timberlake as Sean Parker: “We lived on farms, and then we lived in cities, and now we’re going to live on the internet.” Are we sure Sorkin isn’t clairvoyant?

There’s a fascinating approach the film takes from the outset: it sets out to discover what Zuckerberg’s initial motivations were for creating Facebook, suggesting he did so as a response to ‘the girl who broke his heart’ — something Zuckerberg himself didn’t appreciate, telling an audience at Stanford in 2010 that he disliked the film’s framing of the idea that he made Facebook to get girls. And look — maybe Mark’s right. Maybe he didn’t create Facebook because of a girl. But he’s missing the point. The character of Erica Albright (Rooney Mara), fictitious as she may be, feels authentic regardless, as a microcosm of the pettiness displayed by Zuckerberg and the army of incels his actions would go on to spawn. The girl might not have been real, but the spiteful ‘I’m gonna show you’ attitude she represents very much was. Men really would rather reshape the world for the worse than go to therapy, wouldn’t they?

It’s in this analysis that the film reveals itself to be something truly special — interested not just in understanding the makings of the platform that now runs the world, but in the man behind the wheel. A man whose Adidas sandals have been applying pressure to the pedal at a rate that suggests he places about as much thought into his earth-altering choices as he does his outfit of the day: not a whole lot.

We lived on farms, and then we lived in cities, and now, we’re going to live on the internet.

Regrettably, this is the man most directly responsible for shaping the experience I — the writer of this piece — have had on this earth. As a 22-year-old, Facebook has been around for almost as long as I have, and for as long as I can remember, I’ve had an account. Isn’t that bleak? But it also means that I, and anyone else my age, am exactly the target Zuckerberg’s virtual atomic bomb hit most directly — our childhoods, and our lives as a whole, spent as much online as not. That makes Zuckerberg one of the most important, if not the most important, or at least the most consequential figure alive today, certainly for those of us who have only ever walked the path his actions paved. And the film’s grasp of this, at such an early stage, is what leaves me in awe. It knew something it couldn’t have possibly known yet — that the world we knew had come and gone, that the way of life we’d been accustomed to was no longer tenable, and that the people responsible were a load of facetious, overly sensitive nerds who didn’t fully understand what they were doing. The consequences are still being felt years later, with no sign of resolution on the horizon.

Maybe my favourite piece of evidence for the film’s staying power is the most recent Academy Awards — a ceremony that handed Best Picture to Paul Thomas Anderson’s One Battle After Another, a film that spent almost three hours apologising to the youth of today.

There’s an acceptance, not just at the movies but in the world at large, that the youth of today have never been given the same shot at life that those before them had. Fascists are in charge, employment rates have never been lower, living costs have never been higher, and on top of all that, we’re all addicted to our phones. The generation responsible seems to be feeling a little bad about it. One Battle offered a glimmer of hope in Chase Infiniti’s Willa Ferguson — a young person whose relationship, or lack thereof, with the technology around her is exactly what made her such a symbol of optimism. The sheltered nature of her upbringing — no phones, no social media — felt like Anderson’s way of answering what I’ll call ‘the Facebook problem’: destroy Facebook, maybe. Or at least put the phone down for a while. Go outside. Help someone. Do some good. I don’t know — I think destroying Facebook could work too.

The clear message rising from the films currently winning Best Picture is that our actions have consequences — that the decisions we make can impact the people who come after us to a degree unimaginable in the moment. And yet The Social Network tried to tell us all this sixteen years ago. Does anybody else want to retroactively award this Best Picture too? Can we get that going?

Anyway — whilst we’re here, isn’t the casting great?

Jesse Eisenberg’s casting in particular is still absolutely miraculous. Sure, he was coming off a handful of noteworthy performances, but I’m not sure anyone could’ve known just how well-suited he’d be to this role — so iconic that, for many, the face that comes to mind when you hear the name Mark Zuckerberg isn’t Zuckerberg’s own, but Eisenberg’s. I think that’s because the film’s entire success rests on Eisenberg’s ability to hold the audience at arm’s length. Every time a flicker of humanity starts to show, every time you begin to feel a little bad for Zuckerberg, Eisenberg snatches it away and drops you back into the crushing reality that the men who shaped our future really were just that inconsiderate. The same kind of men now having their own ‘Oppenheimer moment’ with the rise of artificial intelligence, mind you — cough, Sam Altman, cough (look out for Artificial, directed by Luca Guadagnino, next year). Let’s hope those guys learned a thing or two from Zuckerberg’s mistakes — or at least read Dune.

But despite all this, the single greatest indictment of the film’s quality and importance is that now, in 2026, after years of failing to recapture the magic other filmmakers found adapting his scripts, Sorkin is returning to his finest material. His upcoming sequel, The Social Reckoning, starring Mikey Madison, Jeremy Allen White, and Jeremy Strong, depicts the events of the 2021 leak of ‘the Facebook Files’ to the Wall Street Journal by former Facebook data scientist Frances Haugen, which exposed the company’s prioritisation of profit over user safety. It speaks not only to the quality of its predecessor, but to the relevance of the material in today’s cultural moment.

And just look at the numbers. The platform is bigger than ever — as of May 2026, it sits at over 3.07 billion monthly active users, having generated more than $200 billion in revenue in 2025. Its reach grows by the day, and has done since launch — but all you need to do to see that is check the closing credits of The Social Network, where a title card states Facebook was valued at $25 billion at the time. Today, that figure is closer to $1.5 trillion. Facebook, and social media as a whole, has never been in more homes, never affected so many people, and never been as important to the human experience as it is right now. Who knows how long this trajectory continues, or who’s shaping the world of tomorrow, or what the cultural currency will be ten or fifteen years from now? If the prophetic nature of The Social Network is anything to go by, the answers might already be sitting in its sequel.

We are proud to announce that 50% Film has partnered with Schmeckt! Kurzfilmfest — an independent short film festival held in Vienna, created by young filmmakers for young filmmakers, and for anyone who takes cinema seriously.

Schmeckt! is built around a simple premise: that emerging filmmakers deserve a real platform, and that a film school diploma should have nothing to do with getting one. Founded by young creatives in Vienna, the festival screens short work on the big screen at the Gartenbaukino — one of the city's most storied independent cinemas — and surrounds it with exactly the kind of event that short film rarely gets: an art exhibition at InsideOut., a culinary pop-up from dasDrittl, live music, and an afterparty at Celeste that keeps the conversation going long after the credits roll.

For 50% Film, the alignment felt immediate. We launched this label on the conviction that short cinema is worth taking seriously in its own right — not as a calling card, not as a proof of concept, but as a form with its own discipline and its own rewards. Schmeckt! holds the same belief, and builds a community around it. That's rare, and it's worth supporting.

A film school diploma should have nothing to do with getting a platform. Schmeckt! was built on that idea. So were we.

Our partnership supports the festival's programme of screenings and filmmaker events across both festival days. If anything screened at Schmeckt! feels right for the 50% Film platform, we'll be in touch.

If you're a filmmaker with work at Schmeckt! this year, or you're planning to be in the audience — find us on Instagram. We'd love to connect.
